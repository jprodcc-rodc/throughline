# Mom Test Self-Check — Pre + Post Interview

**Author:** user-research-strategist (Tier 0 Task 0c)
**Date:** 2026-05-03
**Purpose:** 10-question yes/no self-check Rodc runs *before* finalizing each interview script and *immediately after* each interview ends. Catches Mom-test failures before they contaminate the next interview.
**Source discipline:** Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test* — past behavior over future intent, specific over abstract, listening over pitching.
**Companion docs:** `user-interview-script.md`, `recruit-strategy.md`.

---

## How to use this

**Before the interview** (5 min, with the script open):
- Run questions 1-5. These check whether the *script as written* will produce real signal.
- If any of 1-5 is "no," edit the script before the interview.

**Immediately after the interview** (10 min, while memory is warm):
- Run questions 6-10. These check whether *what you actually did* in the interview produced real signal.
- If 3+ of 6-10 are "no," the interview is mostly noise. Note that explicitly in your post-interview summary; do not pretend it was strong data.

**Across the 5-interview run**: track the 10-question scores. If a specific question keeps failing across interviews (e.g., #5 "did I follow up on vague answers" is "no" in 4 of 5 interviews), that's the discipline gap to fix in interview 6 onward.

## The 10 Questions

### 1. Did I ask any "would you...", "would you use...", "do you like..." questions? *(Mom-test fail.)*

**Why this matters:** these are future-intent questions. Candidates lie politely about future behavior — not maliciously, just because that's what humans do when asked to predict themselves. The Mom Test core insight: future-intent answers are nearly information-free, past-behavior answers are dense.

**Pre-check:** scan the script. Replace every "would you" with "tell me about the last time you..." or "walk me through what you did when..." Specifically, in Section D, check there is zero "Would you use this?" / "Would you pay for this?" / "Would you sign up?"

**Post-check:** re-listen to the audio (or skim transcript). Did I slip into "would you" anywhere, especially under candidate silence pressure? Note specific moments.

**Pass:** "no" — zero would-you questions in script and execution.

---

### 2. Did the candidate mostly talk about past behavior, or speculate about future preferences? *(>80% past behavior = pass.)*

**Why this matters:** if the candidate is speculating ("I think I'd probably..." / "I imagine..." / "I'd want it to..."), they're predicting themselves. Predictions are unreliable. Reports are reliable.

**Pre-check (script-only, partial):** does each question anchor in a specific past moment? Look for "the last time" / "tell me about when" / "walk me through the last specific moment." Generic "do you usually..." questions invite generic answers.

**Post-check:** estimate the past-behavior vs. future-speculation ratio. Past behavior includes: dated stories, specific count moments, named workflows, specific failed/successful experiences. Future speculation includes: "I'd probably," "I imagine," "users would want," abstract preferences, hypothetical workflows.

**Pass:** post-interview, you estimate >80% of candidate's airtime was past-anchored. **Fail:** <60% past-anchored — interview is mostly imagination, low data value.

---

### 3. Did I pitch the product before probing the problem? *(Skip pitch first 30 minutes.)*

**Why this matters:** once the candidate hears the pitch, they cannot un-hear it. Their answers afterward are filtered through politeness toward the founder and the product. The first 30 minutes must run with zero product context so the candidate's reported workflow is unbiased.

**Pre-check:** confirm Sections A-C contain ZERO mention of Rodix, AI memory features, "what I'm building," continuity-of-thought, or any positioning language. If the script has any pre-Section-D positioning, redact it.

**Post-check:** during the interview, did I get drawn into describing Rodix earlier than Section D — perhaps because the candidate asked "so what's the thing?" Critical: did I answer their what's-the-thing-you're-building question with one sentence ("a memory layer for AI chat — I'll describe more in 30 minutes if that's okay") or did I flip into pitch mode?

**Pass:** "no" — first 30 minutes ran without product context. **Fail:** I described the product in Section A or B — interview's Section C-D data is now contaminated by politeness.

---

### 4. Did I get specific moments, dates, counts, examples? Or did the candidate stay abstract?

**Why this matters:** abstract answers are cheap and meaningless. "I think a lot about hard things" is empty. "Last Tuesday I was working through whether to leave my job, I have 12 paragraphs of voice notes about it" is data. The Mom Test discipline is to push past abstraction every time.

**Pre-check:** does each question have a follow-up plan if the candidate stays abstract? "Walk me through the last time" / "What was the actual situation" / "How long did that last" / "What did you do right after."

**Post-check:** count the specific moments the candidate gave. Specific = a date, a count, a named event, a quoted sentence, a specific other-person, a specific tool. Target: 8-12 specific moments across the 45-60 minute interview. If <5, the interview ran on abstraction.

**Pass:** ≥8 specific moments, post-interview count. **Fail:** <5 — re-evaluate the script's specificity prompts before next interview.

---

### 5. Did I follow up on vague answers ("I think I'd...") with "walk me through the last time"?

**Why this matters:** every interview produces vague answers under social pressure. The Mom-test move is to follow up immediately, every time, without exception. "I think I'd want continuity" is not data. The follow-up "tell me about the last time you wished you had continuity" turns it into data.

**Pre-check:** does the script have explicit "probes when candidate goes vague" sections? (User-interview-script.md does — Section B has 4 stock probes.) Are they accessible to me mid-interview without scrolling?

**Post-check:** count the vague answers I let pass without following up. Target: 0-1. Each let-pass vague answer is data lost.

**Pass:** 0-1 unprobed vague answers. **Fail:** 3+ — I'm letting the candidate skate, probably because of social comfort or interview-fatigue.

---

### 6. Did the candidate compliment the idea? *(Compliments are not signal — they're politeness.)*

**Why this matters:** Mom Test core lesson — compliments are the closest thing to information-zero in user research. "This sounds great" / "I love this idea" / "This would be so useful" are all politeness. None of them predict whether the candidate will use the product or pay for it. If the candidate complimented the idea, your discipline failed (you let yourself respond to politeness as if it were data) OR the candidate is being polite.

**Discipline:** if the candidate compliments, ask "what specifically would you do with it?" Convert the compliment back to behavior territory.

**Post-check:** how many compliments did I receive, and how did I respond to each? Did I bank them as positive signal (mistake) or convert them to behavior questions (correct)?

**Pass:** "no" — candidate did not compliment, or I converted any compliments to behavior probes. **Fail:** "yes, and I felt good about it" — I'm reading politeness as data.

**Honest bonus:** in the post-interview summary, write the strongest compliment the candidate gave AND the strongest behavioral-signal the candidate gave. Compare them. Behavioral signal is the data; the compliment is noise. If they don't agree (candidate complimented but their actual past behavior contradicts the compliment), believe the behavior, not the compliment.

---

### 7. Did I ask "who else should I talk to?" at the end? *(Referrals compound research.)*

**Why this matters:** every interview should produce 1+ referral. Five interviews with referrals each yields 5+ extra candidate leads — research compounds. Five interviews without referrals leaves you cold-recruiting candidate 6 onward.

**Pre-check:** confirm the closing of the script includes the explicit referral question, framed for diversity ("who'd have a different perspective from you?").

**Post-check:** did I ask? Did the candidate name someone? Did I follow up within 24 hours while warmth held?

**Pass:** "yes" — I asked, and within 24 hours of interview ending, I DM'd or noted the referral for next-day outreach. **Fail:** "no" — I forgot. Worth scheduling: every interview ends with a 5-min "post-interview admin" block that includes the referral DM as a checklist item.

---

### 8. Did I record / take real-time notes? *(Memory degrades fast.)*

**Why this matters:** human memory of unstructured conversation degrades by ~50% within 24 hours. Without recording or aggressive note-taking, three days later the interview is mostly an emotional impression, not retrievable detail. Pattern detection across 5 interviews requires retrievable detail.

**Pre-check:** before the interview starts, confirm: is audio recording running (with consent)? OR is a notes doc open and am I willing to type continuously? Pen-and-paper note-taking is acceptable but slower; type if possible.

**Post-check:** do I have either a) audio recording, or b) ≥1500 words of notes from this interview? If I have neither, the interview's data is impressionistic only.

**Pass:** audio recorded OR ≥1500 words of notes. **Fail:** neither — interview is mostly evaporated by 48 hours.

---

### 9. Was the candidate someone the friends-intro target user description fits? *(Verify post-interview, not pre.)*

**Why this matters:** the recruit channel filters for likely fit, but final fit is verified empirically *during the interview* via the four-condition list. A candidate who responds enthusiastically but, on actual conversation, hits 0-1 of the four conditions is anti-target — their interview data is mostly disconfirmation, not validation.

**Pre-check:** N/A — this is a post-only check.

**Post-check:** count how many of the 4 friends-intro conditions the candidate hit empirically (not enthusiastically), based on past-behavior reports in Sections B-C.

| Condition | Empirical hit? |
|---|---|
| 1. Re-explained to ChatGPT for the fifth time and felt the soul-tax | yes / no |
| 2. Used three AIs and wished they shared a brain | yes / no |
| 3. Tried "AI memory" features and got mildly creeped out | yes / no |
| 4. Wanted a record of how their thinking evolved on something hard | yes / no |

**Pass:** ≥2 conditions hit empirically. **Fail:** 0-1 — record this candidate's data with an asterisk in the post-interview summary; they are anti-target. **Important:** anti-target candidates are STILL useful — they disconfirm wedge over-reach. The fail-tag is just signal that their data is disconfirmation, not validation.

---

### 10. What's the most surprising thing this candidate said? *(Surprise = signal.)*

**Why this matters:** anything Rodc could have predicted, Rodc already knew. The interview's information-density is concentrated in the moments where the candidate said something Rodc did not expect. The Mom-test rule of thumb: if you cannot name one surprising thing per interview, either the candidate is anti-target or the script wasn't probing deeply enough.

**Pre-check:** N/A.

**Post-check:** within 30 minutes of interview ending, write down the single most surprising thing the candidate said. One sentence. Specific, not abstract.

Examples of "surprising" written well:
- *"Candidate said they turned ChatGPT memory off after Memory remembered something they'd told it as a joke and surfaced it as a fact two weeks later."* (Specific moment, specific feature, specific failure shape — surfaces V1 / S2 empirically.)
- *"Candidate said they don't think of cards as 'memory' — they think of them as 'receipts of a thinking session.' Used the word 'receipts' three times unprompted."* (Surfaces a vocabulary alternative the friends-intro doesn't use — possible reframe.)

Examples of "surprising" written poorly:
- *"Candidate seemed enthusiastic."* (Vague. Politeness, not signal.)
- *"They liked the cards idea."* (Compliment territory.)

**Pass:** "yes" — concrete surprising sentence written. **Fail:** "I can't think of one" — interview was either anti-target or under-probing. Note explicitly in the post-interview summary; consider re-running the script after the issue is fixed.

**Read pattern:**
- Discipline gaps that are constant across interviews are *me*, not them — fix the script or fix my interview behavior before the next round.
- Discipline gaps that vary are mostly candidate fit — some candidates produce more specific past behavior than others, that's just sampling variance.
- Q9 fail rate is the hardest signal: if 4 of 5 candidates hit <2 conditions empirically, the recruit channel is wrong OR the wedge is genuinely narrower than the friends-intro implies (lean into contradiction D5 / fail mode 2 territory).
- Q10 fail rate is the second-hardest signal: if 4 of 5 interviews produce no surprise, the script is over-leading or the candidates are anti-target. Re-audit script questions for closed-form phrasing.

## The hardest discipline (the one Rodc will most likely fail on)

The single hardest move across the 5-interview run is **letting silence sit**. Mom Test asymmetry is 80% candidate / 20% interviewer, but most first-time interviewers default to 50/50 because silence is socially uncomfortable. The candidate has often not finished their thought; if you fill the silence with the next question, you cut off the most data-dense second of their answer.

**Discipline:** after every candidate answer, count to 4 silently before saying anything. If the candidate is going to add ("...also, the other thing was..."), it almost always happens in seconds 2-4 of silence. If they don't add by second 4, then ask the follow-up.

This is question 0 — pre-check: am I willing to sit in 4 seconds of silence after every candidate answer? Post-check: did I? If not, the next interview should be re-attempted with that single discipline added.
